  ## Starting with the audience


  The strategic work that preceded the build gave us something really useful: a detailed understanding of each of the distinct groups Papyrus needs to reach. Young people in crisis. Parents, friends and carers who are worried about someone. Professionals who work with young people and need resources, training and guidance. Supporters who want to understand the work and contribute to it.


  These groups have different needs, different starting points, different mental states and different relationships with the subject matter. A professional arriving on the site to book a training session has a very different journey than a young person arriving because they're struggling. The architecture of the site had to hold all of that without feeling complicated or cluttered to any single user.


  We mapped those user journeys before anything was designed or built. Structure first, then design, then build. It sounds obvious, but there's always pressure to show something visual early on, and when that pressure wins you tend to end up with a website that looks good and works poorly for the people using it. 


  ## Designing for vulnerability


  The audience that required the most careful thought was the one Papyrus cares most about: young people in crisis. A young person arriving at the site at their most difficult moment is not browsing. They're looking for something specific - hope, a reason to stay, a way to reach out, a signal that help is available and that someone will pick up.


  Every design decision for that user journey was made with that context in mind. Content is structured to surface the most important information quickly and without noise. The visual design is warm and engaging - consistent with the new brand but never so stimulating that it overwhelms. Calls to action, like the HopeLine247 number are clear and direct, not hidden in navigation or buried under secondary content. 


  ## A flexible design system


  One of the things I care about most on any website project is what happens after we hand-over. A site that depends on the agency for every update isn't a useful long-term tool - it's an ongoing liability. The Papyrus website was built with a flexible, component-based design system that gives the in-house team real confidence to publish content without breaking the brand or the user experience.


  The system is structured around clearly defined components, each one designed, documented and tested so the team can build new pages and update existing ones in a way that stays on-brand and on-brief, without needing to make design decisions from scratch every time. That's what gives the site longevity.


  ## Integrations that do real work


  Two integrations were built into the site that are worth talking about specifically, because they each extend the value of the platform in a meaningful way.


  The first is a Stripe-integrated shop. Giving Papyrus a direct route to generate income through merchandise, while giving supporters a simple, tangible way to contribute to the mission.


  The second is CRM integration, connecting the website to Papyrus's CRM system so that data flows automatically rather than relying on manual processes. For a charity team that is always working hard with limited resources, removing that kind of administrative friction is extremely valuable. It means more time spent on the mission and less on data entry.


  ## What we built


  The Papyrus website launched in April 2026. It was built for young people in crisis, for the parents lying awake at night worried about their child, for the friends who don't know what to say or where to turn, and for the people who have lost someone and are trying to make sense of it. What I keep coming back to is this: somewhere, right now, one of those people is on that website. It needs to be clear, calm and easy to navigate. That's what the work was for, and I believe we’ve achieved that.
